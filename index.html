Homeownership Rate

Switzerland has fewer Homeowners than most European Countries

The barchart shows the percentage of inhabitants that actually own their home in 2018. One might be suprised to find one of the financially strongest countries comes in last. Only 42.50% of the swiss are homeowners. Meaning most inhabitants rent their appartments. Until today this ratio has yet to change significantly.

Average House Prices

Europe 2021

One probable reasoning for the above mentioned positioning of Switzerland may be, that its average price for houses (CHF/m2) is higher than most countries. In Switzerland a buyer pays on average over 7000 CHF per square meter which is over 1.6 times the second most expensive country Norway.

Real Estate Prices in Switzerland are higher than ever

The Prices for buying Real Estate are increasing at a higher rate than they are for renting

The already extremely high price of becoming a homeowner in switzerland is constantly increasing. The lineplot shows the development of the price per square meter since 2020. (01-01-2020 = 100%) Whilst the prices for rental appartments have 'only' increased by 5.2%, for condominiums and houses they have increased by approximatly 19% since 2020. So condominiums and houses getting more expensive faster than rental appartments.

Single Family Homes Region

Cut-Off Year: 2021

The following plot shows the number of Inhabitants and the fraction of Single Family Homes in respect to the total number of buildings for each canton. When investigating this regional aspect, one can make several observations:

Cantons of Central Switzerland: All these cantons have lower proportion of Single Family Homes (below 50 % of the total number of buildings). Cantons of Eastern Switzerland and Lake Geneva Region: These cantons show the exact opposite to the cantons of Central Switzerland. The proportion of Single Family Homes is in general over 50 %. Cantons of Northwestern Switzerland: The two half cantons of Basel (BL: land, BS: City) are both at the very extrem when it comes to the percentage of Single Family Homes. Big Cities: The two cantons of Zurich (ZH) and Bern (BE) are obvious outliers in this plot. Due to their big cities (e.g., Zurich, Winterthur, Bern, Thun) they lay far more on the right side of the plot and stand out compared to the other cantons. These observations underline some facts that we already know:

Firstly, the Cantons of Eastern Switzerland are known to be more rural and focused on agriculture and farming. Cities and villages are relatively small and less populated. This leads to the areas being less in demand and therefore, the price of land might be considerably more affordable when compared to other cantons (see more on that later -> Sabrina).

Secondary, Cantons like Zug (ZG) have a lower tax load so hounding tends to be more expensive (see more on that later -> Sabrina).

Costs and Financial Challenges

Analysis of Financial Aspects, Tax Burden, and Feasibility of Property Purchase in the Context of the Swiss Real Estate Market: A Focus on the Canton of Zurich

The Swiss real estate market is renowned for its high prices and low homeownership rates. This section is dedicated to an analysis of the financial aspects, including the tax burden, associated with purchasing a property, with a specific focus on the canton of Zurich.

The aim of this analysis is to provide a comprehensive assessment of the financial burden and feasibility of property acquisition in the canton of Zurich. The impact of the tax burden on overall costs and long-term sustainability will be critically examined to highlight potential risks and challenges.

Relevant Taxes for purchasing property

Land Registry/Transfer Tax When purchasing a property, stamp duty is levied depending on the regulations of the respective county, occasionally also by the municipalities. Where the tax is levied by the county, the municipalities are often involved in the tax revenue or can impose (limited) surcharges to the county tax. In certain counties, the municipalities have the authority, but not the obligation, to levy a stamp duty («optional» municipal tax).

Note: Stamp duty is levied in addition to the land registry fees, which are subject to the principles of equivalence and cost recovery. Specifically, the counties have the following provisions. The amount is usually around 1 to 3 percent of the purchase price. The responsibility for payment can lie with either the seller or the buyer. Property/Real Estate Tax Property tax is levied in about half of the counties. It is usually around 2 to 3 per mil of the estimated property value. The owners or co-owners according to the land register are liable to pay the tax. Wealth Tax Wealth tax is levied on the value of real estate and land. It applies in all Swiss counties and municipalities. The calculation is based on the taxable value of the property, and the exact calculation method may vary depending on the county. Mortgage debts can be deducted from the wealth value. Income Tax For owner-occupied properties, income tax is relevant. The imputed rental value is added to the taxable income. However, deductions for mortgage interest, maintenance expenses, and contributions to the 3rd pillar can be made to reduce the taxable income.

Deductions and Tax-Reducing factors

Maintenance The costs for maintaining a property can be claimed as tax deductions. This includes repairs, renovations, and maintenance work. It is important to keep receipts and invoices for these expenses in order to submit them with the tax return. Insurance Premiums Certain insurance premiums such as building insurance, glass insurance, water damage insurance, fire insurance, and liability insurance are tax-deductible. Imputed Rental Value For owner-occupied residential property, the imputed rental value is taken into account. This value is usually about 60 to 70 percent of the rental value of a comparable property. The imputed rental value is considered as income and is subject to taxation. However, mortgage interest and maintenance costs can be deducted from this value, which has a tax-reducing effect. The imputed rental value of a self-occupied property can be reduced, for example, when a child moves out. It should be noted that the previously occupied rooms must actually remain vacant in order to claim the lower imputed rental value. Amortisation As a mortgage borrower, there is the possibility to make contributions to the 3rd pillar, which are tax-deductible up to an annual maximum amount. This can be particularly beneficial for owner-occupied residential property.
